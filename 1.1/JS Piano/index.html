JS Piano Tutorial

Play the JS Piano by typing numbers and letters. Hold shift and type a key to play a sharp.
Disclaimer: JS Piano was created to work on the lastest version of Google Chrome. There is no guarantee that all features work in other browsers.

Change the major you want to play in by clicking the display and clicking a scale.
If you have an odd-ball scale, create a custom scale.
Or you can cycle by using the shortcuts [SHIFT + ,] and [SHIFT + .].

You can focus one key by clicking on it.
Focused keys can be played by pressing [ENTER].
You can cycle focus by using the left and right arrow buttons.
Another way to cycle is by using [TAB] and [SHIFT + TAB].

As you play some more difficult songs, you may realize that some keys cannot be played simultaneously.
This is because your keyboard doesn't have "n-key rollover." N-key rollover allows your keyboard to correctly handle several simultaneous keystrokes.
Luckily, most mechanical keyboards have n-key rollover support. Google about your mechanical keyboard to see how to turn on n-key rollover.

On the sheets tab, you can click on the dropdown menu to bring up a list of preset music sheets.
To the right of the dropdown menu, you can change the page number if the sheet is multiple pages long.
To change pages quickly, use the shortcuts [SHIFT + =] and [=].

Want to learn how to read JS Piano sheets, or make your own sheets? Check out the Notation tab for more information.

This project was inspired by Crystal Magic Studio's Virtual Piano.

JS Piano Notation

In JS piano, we use very simple notation. Let's see by example:

In the first musical line of the Marble Machine sheet, we have
[0f]... [wr].a. .... [wr].p.
Chords and notes are played on the keyboard. In this example ([0f] . [wr] a p) are chords and notes.
To be precise, [0f] and [wr] are chords and the rest are notes.
The meaning of [0f] is that the notes 0 and f are to be played simultaneously.
We use one or more periods in order to signify rests. (Remember that rests are counted as notes!)
Spaces don't mean anything, they're there to help organize the chords and notes into easily digestible groups.

Let's look at the first line:
-G major, 125 beats of 4 notes per minute
On the virtual piano, every chord or note count as beats. This way, you can easily keep time.
This example says that if you count every 4 notes as a beat, there should be 125 beats per minute.
This means every 4 beats should take about half a second to play.
In the case of Marble Machine, chords and notes are grouped by spaces in such a way so that 2 groups should take about a second to play.

As go down the song, you may notice that there are some lines where there are dashes followed by a number and then some more dashes:
-0 ---
The dash at the start shows that this line is a comment and should not be played. The number is the current measure (music terminology) you're at.
The purpose of these measures is to help you quickly locate where you were after you've changed the page or after you've glanced at your keyboard.

As you play more advanced songs, you may notice notes such as these:
{[5w]yo}...
The brackets mean that this chord has a grace note. Realize that chords with grace notes are also counted as one beat.
This means that you have to split the time it takes to play one beat into the three notes inside the bracket.
[5w] gets 1/3 of the time, y gets 1/3 of the time, and o gets 1/3 of the time.
To be precise, the chord is played immediately, the first note is played after 1/3 of a beat, and the last note is played after 2/3 of a beat.
The point is to get all the chords and all the notes to be played for the same amount of time.

When writing your own custom sheet, it's necessary that you write every chord and note in terms of the fastest type of note in the piece of music you're transcribing.
If you want to force a new page, you can add some new lines, or you can use tilde by itself on a new line. This character is the tilde: ~
